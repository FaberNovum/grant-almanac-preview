Foreword

In December 2021, a designer of systems began sharing wisdom on Twitter. Over the next three years, Grant Lannin would post thousands of observations about money, relationships, personal growth, and the architecture of a well-lived life. Each tweet was a small seed—deceptively simple, often poetic, always intentional.

This almanac is what happened when we planted all those seeds in the same garden and watched to see what grew.

The Making of This Book

What you’re holding is an experiment in applied knowledge synthesis. We ingested over 2,700 tweets through the X API, mapped them into a hypergraph database using hypabase, identified the conceptual threads that connected them, and let the structure emerge organically. The result: fifteen interconnected chapters spanning 5,600+ connections between ideas.

This isn’t a traditional book where one person sits down to write from beginning to end. It’s something stranger and perhaps more honest—a map of a mind in motion, rendered visible through the mathematics of connection. Where Grant’s ideas clustered densely, chapters formed. Where concepts bridged across domains, we found the throughlines that make his philosophy coherent.

What You’ll Find Here

The chapters flow from the internal to the external, from foundation to expression:

Chapters 1-7 build the inner architecture—mindset, self-awareness, authenticity, and the systems that support sustainable growth. Grant’s philosophy begins with the self, but not the self as isolated ego. Rather, the self as system: observable, designable, improvable.

Chapters 8-11 explore how we move through the world—relationships, productivity, psychology, and the management of our finite energy. Here you’ll find Grant’s most practical wisdom, grounded in the reality that we’re always in relationship: to others, to our work, to time itself.

Chapters 12-15 expand to the existential—life philosophy, decision-making, spirituality, and the deeper questions of meaning and purpose. This is where Grant’s systems thinking meets his evident love for the human experience in all its complexity.

The chapter on Money Mindset sits deliberately at the center, bridging the personal and the practical. Because in Grant’s world, money is never just money—it’s energy, freedom, stored time, and a reflection of values.

The Spirit of the Work

“Designer of systems, lover of you.” Grant’s Twitter bio captures something essential. His philosophy is both rigorously structural and deeply humanistic. He believes in optimization and in grace. In discipline and in surrender. In building better systems and in leaving space for mystery.

Reading through the corpus of his work, certain patterns emerge: an almost spiritual reverence for simplicity, a conviction that most problems are design problems, and an unwavering belief that people can change—not through force, but through the patient redesign of the systems that shape them.

His voice is distinctive: direct without being harsh, wise without pretension, systematic without losing sight of the human. He writes like someone who has done the work and continues to do it.

A Living Document

This almanac captures Grant’s philosophy as of its publication, synthesized from tweets through early 2025. But Grant continues to post, think, and evolve. Each new tweet enters the knowledge graph, potentially creating new connections, strengthening existing themes, or revealing entirely new territories.

Future editions will reflect this growth. Consider this Volume 1 of an ongoing project—a snapshot of a living philosophy, certain to deepen and expand as more data flows in and the graph reveals new patterns.

How to Use This Book

You can read it cover to cover, or you can dive into whichever chapter calls to you. The chapters are interconnected—just like the tweets that built them—so you’ll find threads that pull you from one domain to another. That’s by design. Grant’s philosophy resists neat compartmentalization because life itself does.

What matters most is that you engage with the ideas, test them against your own experience, and use what serves you. Grant would want nothing less.

This is his garden. Walk through it slowly. Notice what blooms.

Chapter 1: Mindset

Your life is exactly what your mind will allow it to be. Not what circumstances allow. Not what your résumé allows. Not what your past allows. What your mind allows.

And right now, your mind is probably fucking with you.

It’s telling you things are harder than they are. It’s inventing competitions that don’t exist. It’s manufacturing hesitations about things you actually want. It’s running psychological programs in the background that quietly sabotage everything you touch. Most people spend their entire lives imprisoned by these invisible barriers, never once questioning whether the walls are even real.

They’re not.

The Intelligence Trap

Here’s something most people get backwards:

“Balls + dumb = successful. Smart + balls = hypersuccessful. Smart + no balls = monstrously depressed. IQ prob isn’t ur problem”

Intelligence without courage is a curse. You’re smart enough to see all the ways things could go wrong. Smart enough to calculate risk with precision. Smart enough to generate sophisticated reasons why you shouldn’t move. And all that intelligence just builds a more elaborate prison.

The skills you actually need aren’t the ones you think. When normies think of “skills,” they only conceptualize physical-world related things—the hard skills, the credentials, the technical knowledge. But the skills that determine whether you win or lose are internal: emotional regulation, creating a vision for yourself, managing your own energy, reframing internal narratives, policing your own thought patterns.

Most people are trying to develop the wrong skills entirely. They’re taking courses and collecting certificates while the real battle—the one that determines everything—is happening inside their own head.

“Ur life will 100x in speed when u stop being afraid to guess. What’s answer here? Just make ur best guess & go. Normies think (probably because of school brainwashing) there is a ‘magical correct answer out there that I don’t know’. Nope. It always just feels like a guess.”

School trained you to believe there’s always a correct answer and that not knowing it makes you inadequate. Real life doesn’t work like that. The most successful people aren’t the ones who wait until they’re certain—they’re the ones who got comfortable with the fact that it always feels like guessing. They just guess faster and adjust as they go.

Soul-Level Commitment

There’s a difference between wanting something intellectually and wanting it at a soul level. Most people have never experienced the second one.

“When you truly want it, you’ll get it. Ur kinda worried now that u might not get it but its more of an intellectual worry. Not a soul worry. U haven’t TRULY decided cuz u don’t TRULY want it yet. The moment you truly decide, it will come quicker & more effortlessly than you”

You say you want things. You make plans. You set goals. But underneath, there’s hesitation. Internal resistance. A part of you that isn’t sure, that wants to keep escape routes open, that’s protecting yourself from potential failure or embarrassment.

“Nothing is hard to get if you GENUINELY, at a soul level, commit to getting it. Issue is the % of people that have ever soul-committed to anything is like .00000000000000001%. This is most people’s ‘biggest issue’—inability to clear out all of the internal hesitancies”

Soul-level commitment means no backup plans. No mental escape hatches. No “well, if this doesn’t work out…” It means making something so non-negotiable that your entire being reorganizes around it. The moment you do this—the moment you truly decide—reality starts conforming to match.

“The second you make an absolute decision about something is the second the universe starts enacting it for you. God hides the magic of his intelligent design behind a declaration of 100% conviction. Only pure, absolute certainty at the core of you unlocks it. An amazing feature.”

This isn’t mysticism. It’s psychology. When you’re genuinely committed, you stop self-sabotaging. You stop generating reasons to quit. You stop looking for permission. Your brain shifts from “should I?” to “how?” and suddenly everything that seemed impossible becomes a logistics problem.

The Competition Illusion

“There is genuinely zero competition to anything. It’s actually fucking hilarious. If you give even the slightest fuck about anything you’ll run laps. If you press the absolute tippy-tippy-top of ur toe to the gas the world just crumbles in front of u & gives u what u want.”

Most people don’t actually try. They go through motions. They do the minimum. They protect their ego by never fully committing, so if they fail, they can tell themselves “well, I wasn’t really trying anyway.”

You’re competing against that. Against people who are protecting themselves from their own potential. Against people who care more about looking good than being good. Against people whose self-image won’t allow them to appear foolish, so they never attempt anything that might make them look stupid.

“The people who ‘make it’ are usually hyper-savage in a way that a normie mind simply can’t conceptualize. Especially because these people can put on the ‘blend in’ facade & will come off to you as semi-normal & relatable. But u better believe that person is aggressively softening”

The winners you see aren’t magical. They’re just willing to be more aggressive, more persistent, more shameless about going after what they want. They’ve accepted something you haven’t yet: that getting what you want requires you to be willing to look ridiculous, fail publicly, and push through anyway.

The Shame Threshold

This is where most people break.

“If you can just accept the fact everything you want will require you to go through a temporary shamerape then you can quite literally do anything. It will drench your entire being & it will fucking suck but it’s only temporary and on the other side of it is the exact thing you want”

Every meaningful thing you want sits on the other side of potential embarrassment. Starting a business means risking public failure. Pursuing someone means risking rejection. Creating anything means risking criticism. Your mind magnifies this risk into something catastrophic, when in reality it’s just temporary discomfort.

“Your life will rise in direct proportion to the amount of ‘potential shame’ you can put on the chopping block & slice through with power & tenacity. Have to avoid tip-toeing towards what u want & drop nuts on counter to get it. Let cards fall where they may.”

The equation is simple: The more willing you are to risk looking stupid, the more you can attempt. The more you can attempt, the more you can achieve. Your tolerance for temporary embarrassment is the constraint on your entire life.

“If you remove shame around your desires & just stop suppressing them while openly going after them your life gets a lot better. No need to act like you’re a tame little choir boy. We are all beasts.”

You’re pretending to be smaller than you are because you think the real you is too much. Too intense, too ambitious, too weird, too aggressive, too something. You’ve learned to soften yourself, to blend in, to be palatable. And in doing so, you’ve locked away the exact energy that would make you magnetic and unstoppable.

“U run from urself cuz u think if u enact ur full self it will be too much for people. Fuck it. Flip ur psyche. Go from ‘im scared to stand out’ to ‘watch me stand out’. ‘I dont wanna rock the boat’ to ‘imma rock the boat so hard its gonna be a troll’. Lean IN, not out.”

The Uncertainty Feature

Most people think uncertainty is the enemy. It’s actually the entire point.

“If u knew how everything would play out u’d never grow. Massive uncertainty (what people fear the most) is quite literally the single best aspect/feature in the entire fucking game. Throw urself in & play”

The not-knowing is where all the growth happens. The discomfort of “I don’t know if this will work” is the sensation of becoming someone new. If everything was guaranteed, you’d never develop courage, resourcefulness, or resilience. You’d never discover what you’re capable of.

“The secret is really just being unafraid to fuck up, unafraid to admit you’re wrong, unafraid to admit you don’t know what you’re doing. And to then push through despite all of that and learn everything you need to learn until you get there. Feeling like ‘uhhh i don’t really know’ is the ENTIRE game”

Successful people aren’t the ones who figured it all out before they started. They’re the ones who were willing to look clueless, fuck up repeatedly, and keep moving anyway. They’ve made peace with not knowing. More than that—they’ve realized that “I don’t know, but I’ll figure it out” is actually the most powerful position you can take.

“The ‘secret’ is when you jump, the net appears. But in reality there’s not really a jump & a net isn’t really even required. What your mind creates/perceives as an ‘infinite fall into a never-ending abyss of unknown & scary’ is actually nothing. U step outside of it & realize”

Your mind dramatizes risk. It takes a small step and projects it as catastrophe. But when you actually move, you discover the horror was imagined. The fall you feared doesn’t exist. You just step into a slightly uncomfortable new situation and… handle it. Like you always do.

The Confidence Equation

“NEVER lose your fucking confidence. You’re an imperfect person operating forward courageously through the darkness of infinite guesses where every wrong move has heavy consequences. Most simply not built for that shit. But YOU are. Built different fr fr. Forgive yourself for all”

Confidence isn’t about being certain you’ll succeed. It’s about being certain you can handle whatever happens. It’s trusting yourself to figure things out, to recover from failures, to adapt when things go wrong.

Most people lose confidence because they tie it to outcomes. They tried something, it didn’t work, so they conclude they’re inadequate. But the real confidence comes from knowing you’re the kind of person who keeps trying until something works.

“If you view the world in terms of ‘shots on goal’, L’s basically cease to exist. If something doesn’t work out in THAT MOMENT it’s either the percentages running themselves out or it’s in progress & is on its way to victory. ‘The win has been slightly delayed’. More shots”

This is the reframe that changes everything. You’re not failing—you’re collecting data. You’re not losing—you’re still in the game. Every attempt that doesn’t work is just proof you’re actually trying, which puts you ahead of 99% of people who are still preparing to prepare.

Taking Your Power Back

The most important mindset shift is simple: stop blaming anything outside yourself.

“Train yourself to never blame anything outside of yourself, ever. The moment you catch yourself blaming anything that isn’t you—immediately ask ‘but where am i failing here & how can I get better? What do i need to learn more about and get better at?’ YOU are only thing you control”

External blame feels good temporarily. It protects your ego. But it also makes you powerless. If your problems are someone else’s fault, you can’t fix them. You’re waiting for the world to change, for people to treat you differently, for circumstances to improve.

“If you replaced every excuse you ever make with the core question(s) of ‘how is this problem resulting from a leadership issue on my end & what can i do right now to patch up this problem, no matter how poorly?’ You’ll 10x your life in 12 calendar months. Take your power back.”

When you assume everything is your fault—not in a self-flagellating way, but in an empowering way—you suddenly have leverage. You can’t control the economy, other people, or circumstances. But you can control how you think, what you do, and how you respond. That’s actually all you need.

The Ultimate Reframe

At the core of Grant’s philosophy on mindset is one radical possibility:

“WHAT IF IT WORKS OUT MORE PERFECTLY THAN YOU EVER FUCKING IMAGINED”

Your mind is biased toward catastrophizing. It’s generating worst-case scenarios constantly, trying to protect you from potential threats. But it’s equally possible—maybe more possible—that things work out better than you can currently conceptualize.

“What if perfection magically inserts itself into your life in nooks & crannies that you couldn’t even previously conceptualize? Beauty & intelligent design goes beyond the conscious mind. Have faith in beauty”

What if the risk you’re afraid to take leads to the exact life you want? What if the thing you’re procrastinating on is the door to everything? What if you’re one decision away from a completely different trajectory?

“You have so much potential. Don’t allow yourself to squander it. Get pissed & force your dreams to conform to your will. Nobody will hand you the map. U gotta draw it with a shitty crayon & then execute like a fucking savage. U have everything u need, just gotta send it”

You already have everything you need. The resources, the intelligence, the capability—it’s all there. The only thing standing between you and the life you want is your willingness to act despite uncertainty, to tolerate temporary discomfort, to trust yourself enough to jump.

Your mindset isn’t a fixed thing you’re stuck with. It’s a tool you can sharpen. A lens you can adjust. A set of programs you can rewrite.

The question is: will you?

Key Principles

  • Intelligence without courage is depression. Your IQ isn’t your problem—your unwillingness to act despite uncertainty is.

  • Soul-level commitment unlocks reality. True decision-making isn’t intellectual—it’s eliminating all internal hesitation and making something non-negotiable.

  • Shame tolerance determines success. Everything you want is on the other side of potential embarrassment. Your willingness to risk looking stupid is the constraint on your entire life.

  • External blame is powerlessness. The moment you take full responsibility for everything, you gain the leverage to change everything.

  • Uncertainty is the feature, not the bug. The discomfort of not knowing is where all growth happens. Being comfortable with “I’ll figure it out” is the real skill.
